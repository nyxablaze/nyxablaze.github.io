1. Week 7 - Pages 646 - 664, 666 - 674 (Psychological Disorders chapter 15)

1.1. Psychotherapy

The basic goal of all psychotherapy, no matter the approach, is to help people change their thoughts, feelings and behaviour patterns, so they can live happier and more productive lives.

In Australia, one of the most important groups of professionals providing psychological treatment are Clinical Psychologists. Typically, Clin psychs have a Doctor of Clinical Psych or a Master of Clinical Psych and at least 6 years of intensive training and supervision in psychological assesment, research and therapy. Some Clinical Psychs may also hold a Doctor of Philosophy. Psychiatrists, on the other hand, are medical doctors who specialise in psychotherapy and biomedical treatments i.e. drug therapy.

1.1.1. Treatments for psychological disorders

There are two major types of treatments, with subcategories for each.

  1. Psychological
    1. Psychodynamic

      Involves Psychoanalysis, popularised by Freud. Includes Brief psychodynamic therapies.

      1. Psychoanalysis

        Psychoanalysis -> the specific approach to treatment that Freud developed. The goal being, to achieve insight (the conscious awareness of the psychodynamics that underlie their problems).

        • This awareness permits patients to adjust behaviours to their current situations, rather than repeat maladaptive behaviours learned in childhood.
        • As the patient repeatedly encounters and deals with repressed emotions, motives and conflicts within and outside therapy, the energy that was previously given to keeping unconscious conflicts under control can be redirected to more adaptive behaviours.
        • “Where there was ID, there shall ego be” - Freud
        1. Treatment Types
          1. Free Association

            Clients verbally report without censorship any thoughts, feelings or images that enter their awareness. Analysts sit out of sight so the client’s thought processes are all internal. The analyst does not always expect the technique to lead directly to unconscious material, but instead hopes that it will provide clues concerning important themes or issues. E.g., a client may suddenly stop having a stream of thoughts after mentioning her father, suggesting she was approaching a loaded topic and activated repressive defences.

          2. Dream Interpretation

            Self explanatory

          3. Resistance

            some patients may resist during therapy as defensive manoeuvers that hinder the process of therapy. Usually means anxiety-arousing material is being approached.

          4. Transference

            When the patient irrationally transfers onto the therapist either positively or negatively based on past experiences.

          5. Interpretation

            Any statement by the therapist that is intended to provide the client with insight into his or her behaviour or dynamics.

    2. Humanistic

      Person centred therapy, popularised by Carl Rogers. Focuses on the idea that humans are capable of change, and the patient is given a space to explore themselves, rather than given solutions by the psych.

      1. Gestalt Therapy

        Organised whole utilises a variety of imaginative techniques to help clients get in touch with their inner selves. performed in grouops.

    3. Cognitive

      Rational-emotive therapy (Ellis, ABCD) and Cognitive therapy (Beck).

      1. ABCD

        A(ctivating) B(elief) C(onsequences) D(isputing and changing of B)

    4. Behavioural

      Classical Conditioning

      • exposure
      • systematic desensitisation
      • aversion therapy

      Operant Conditioning

      • positive reinforcement
      • punishment
      • behavioural activation

      Modelling

      • social skills training

      ’Third wave’ cognitive behavioural

      • mindfulness based treatments
      • Acceptance and commitment therapy
      • Dialectical behaviour therapy
  2. Biological
    1. Drug Therapy

      Antidepressents, Antipsychotics, Anxiolytics

    2. ECT (electro-convulsive therapy)

      Induced seizers, high initial response (60 - 70%), high relapse rates, may cause brain damage if repeated frequently

    3. Psycho-surgery

2.3. Types of reliability and validity in psychological Testing

Types of reliability Meaning and critical questions
Test-retest reliability Are scores on the measure stable over time?
Internal Consistency Do all of the items on the measure seem to be measuring the same thing as indicated by high correlations among them?
Interjudge reliability Do different raters or scorers agree on their scoring or observations?
Types of validity  
construct validity Do the questions of test items relate to all aspects of the construct being measured?
content validity To what extent is the test actually measuring the construct of interest?
Criterion-related validity Do scores on the test predict some present or future behaviour or outcome assumed to be affected by the construct being measured?

3. Week 9 - Pages 502 - 504, 517 - 525, 526 - 534, 538 - 545 (personality chapter 13 summary)

3.1. Personality

Personality is the enduring patterns of thinking, feeling, and acting that characterize an individual’s responses to life situations. It has internal causes and meaningful organization.

3.2. Psychodynamic Perspective

3.2.1. Freud’s Theory

Personality dynamics involve conflicts between the id, ego, and superego. Defense mechanisms are used to cope with anxiety and instinctual urges. Personality development occurs through oral, anal, and phallic stages.

3.2.2. Neoanalytic Theories

Emphasize social and cultural factors. Object relations theory focuses on mental representations.

3.3. Phenomenological-Humanistic Perspective

3.3.1. Humanistic Theories

Emphasize subjective experiences and personal constructs of reality. Rogers’ theory highlights the self and self-actualization.

3.3.2. Self-Concept

Research explores self-esteem, self-enhancement, self-verification, and character strengths.

3.4. Trait Perspective

3.4.1. Identifying Traits

Trait theorists aim to identify and measure basic personality dimensions. Factor analysis is used.

3.4.2. Cross-Situational Consistency

Traits are not highly consistent across situations, and self-monitoring tendencies play a role.

3.5. Biological Foundations of Personality

3.5.1. Genetics and Environment

Genetics contribute to about half of personality test variance. Evolutionary theories link personality to natural selection.

3.5.2. Eysenck’s Theory

Introversion-extraversion and stability-instability reflect arousal levels.

3.6. Social-Cognitive Perspective

3.6.1. Reciprocal Determinism

Behavior results from interactions between individuals, their behavior, and the environment.

3.6.2. Rotter’s Expectancy Theory

Behavior is influenced by expectancies and reinforcement values.

3.6.3. Bandura’s Self-Efficacy

Relates to one’s perceived ability to achieve goals in specific situations.

3.6.4. Mischel and Shoda’s CAPS

Individual differences in encoding strategies, expectancies, and emotions influence behavior.

  1. Expectancies and beliefs
  2. Goals and values
  3. Encoding strategies
  4. Affects
  5. Competencies and self-regulation processes

3.7. Culture, Gender, and Personality

3.7.1. Cultural Differences

Cultures vary in complexity, tightness, and individualism-collectivism, impacting personality development.

3.7.2. Gender Schemas

Organized mental structures define attributes and behaviors considered appropriate for males and females.

3.8. Personality Assessment

3.8.1. Methods

Psychologists use interviews, behavioral assessments, personality scales, and projective tests.

3.8.2. Personality Scales

Constructed through rational-theoretical or empirical approaches.

3.8.3. Projective Tests

Rorschach and Thematic Apperception Test use ambiguous stimuli to reveal internal processes.

4. Week 10 and 11 - pages 696 - 704 (social chapter 17)

4.1. Social Thinking

4.1.1. Attribution Bias

We tend to underestimate situational factors in others’ behavior and show self-serving bias in our own successes and failures.

4.1.2. First Impressions

They carry extra weight and are influenced by stereotypes and schemas.

4.1.3. Attitudes and Behavior

Attitudes predict behavior best when situational influences are weak, the attitude is strong, and we consciously think about it. Behavior can also influence attitudes through cognitive dissonance.

4.1.4. Persuasion

Communicator credibility, fear arousal, and the central or peripheral route to persuasion.

4.2. Social Influence

4.2.1. Conformity

Influenced by informational and normative social influence. Conformity levels are affected by the majority’s size and the presence of a dissenter. Milgram’s research on obedience.

4.2.2. Compliance Techniques

The norm of reciprocity and techniques like door-in-the-face, foot-in-the-door, and lowballing.

4.2.3. Social Loafing

Occurs in group settings when individual effort decreases. Decreases with high goal value and performance monitoring.

4.2.4. Group Decision-Making

Group polarization and groupthink.

4.2.5. Deindividuation

Occurs in groups or crowds, often due to anonymity to outsiders.

4.3. Social Relations

4.3.1. Attraction

Proximity, mere exposure, similarity, and physical attractiveness enhance attraction. Social exchange theory and evolutionary and sociocultural perspectives on mate preferences.

4.3.2. Ostracism

Produces negative psychological consequences and activates brain regions associated with physical pain.

4.3.3. Prejudice

Stemming from in-group and out-group perception, in-group favoritism, and an out-group homogeneity bias. Ways to reduce prejudice.

4.3.4. Prosocial Behavior

Influenced by genetic predisposition, social learning, and bystander intervention factors.

4.3.5. Aggression

Influenced by heredity and various situational factors. Media violence’s effect on aggression.

5. Week 12 - pages 745 - 764, 769 - 772 (cross-cultural chapter 18)

5.1. Understand why it is important to look at cross-cultural and Indigenous issues

5.1.1. Appreciating Professional Development

Examining cross-cultural and Indigenous issues helps us understand the development of psychology as a profession and how these issues have evolved over time.

5.1.2. Reflecting on Cultural Identity

Emphasizes critical reflection on our cultural makeup and its implications for practice and research. These reflective practices can become integral to a psychologist’s ongoing development.

5.2. Describe the skills and knowledge that form the base of cross-cultural psychology

5.2.1. Foundation of Cross-Cultural Psychology

Skills in critical reflection, understanding cultural competence, and addressing power dynamics in intercultural contexts are foundational. Cultural competence is not a straightforward solution but a complex concept.

5.3. Define multiculturalism and ’race’ and understand how Australia has worked toward being a multicultural society

5.3.1. Multiculturalism and ’Race’

Multiculturalism involves multiple cultures coexisting within a country, while ’race’ traditionally relies on phenotypical characteristics. ’Race’ is increasingly viewed as a social construction rather than a biological concept. Australia’s multicultural identity is challenged by policies requiring conformity to dominant cultural norms.

5.4. Evaluate how we look at the similarities and differences between cultures and understand the implications of these differences

5.4.1. Approaches to Cultural Differences

Researchers use etic (commonalities or differences across cultures) and emic (specific psychological aspects of a culture) perspectives. It is essential to recognize cultural differences, develop cultural competence, and avoid assuming universality, especially in Australia’s multicultural context.

5.5. Define Indigenous psychology and understand how psychologists work with Indigenous Australians

5.5.1. Indigenous Psychology

Refers to psychologies rooted in local sociocultural and geographical realities, emphasizing psychologists’ connection to their context.

5.5.2. Ambiguous Position

Psychologists may occupy an ambivalent or misunderstood position in the lives of Indigenous Australians. Awareness and explanation of psychology’s role are crucial. Reconciliation with history is necessary to understand the profession’s legacy in cross-cultural engagement.

5.6. Describe how Indigenous research is produced and by whom

5.6.1. Indigenous Research

Refers to research involving Indigenous people, with a growing focus on beneficence for participants. Indigenous Australian involvement in all research phases is increasing, emphasizing the benefits to Indigenous communities and the role of Indigenous people in shaping research questions and interpretations. Indigenous research involves collaboration and Indigenous direction in research processes.
